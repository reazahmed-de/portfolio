Campaign Learning System

Why I built it and what it's useful for

When campaigns don't perform as expected, teams often move on quickly without really reflecting on what happened. I noticed that feedback, results, and assumptions usually stay scattered across messages, dashboards, or meeting notes. Over time, the same mistakes tend to repeat.

I built this Campaign Learning System to keep campaign reviews simple and structured, without turning them into long reports or academic documents.

The idea was not to judge success or failure, but to answer a few clear questions:

  • What did we expect to happen?
  • What actually happened?
  • Which signals did we miss or misunderstand?
  • What should we do differently next time?

The system is intentionally lightweight. Each campaign is reviewed using the same structure, focusing on clarity rather than metrics overload. Instead of listing many possible causes, it forces the selection of one main root cause. This helps keep discussions focused and avoids vague conclusions.

What I found useful while building this is how it shifts attention from explaining the past to improving future decisions. The final outcome is always a short rule or takeaway that can be reused in the next campaign.

This project reflects how I approach marketing work: structured, practical, and focused on learning loops rather than perfect outcomes. I designed it at a scope that fits student and junior roles, where execution and clarity matter more than complex strategy.

First-Time User Journey Map

Understanding where users struggle before first value

Many products explain what they can do very well, but new users still struggle to get started. While exploring Notion as a first-time user, I noticed that most confusion does not come from missing features, but from not knowing what to do next.

I built this First-Time User Journey Map to better understand how users move from their first website visit to the moment they actually experience value. The goal was not to redesign the product, but to clearly see where hesitation and uncertainty appear along the way.

The journey focuses on one user type and one path only. Each step documents what the user does, what the product shows, and how the user might feel at that moment. Instead of assumptions, I tried to capture realistic questions users ask themselves, such as:

  • "Where do I start?"
  • "Am I using this correctly?"
  • "What should I do next?"
  • "Is this tool actually right for me?"

What stood out during this exercise was how late value is actually felt. Even after signing up and opening the product, users often remain unsure until they complete a small but meaningful action. This gap between access and value is where many users drop off.

This project helped me think more clearly about activation, messaging, and guidance from a product marketing perspective. It shows how small moments of clarity can make a big difference for first-time users, especially in flexible tools like Notion.

Working Student Toolkit

A practical system to stay reliable and keep learning

When working as a student in a team, it's often unclear what "doing a good job" actually means. Tasks come from different people, priorities change, and questions sometimes stay unasked because you don't want to interrupt or slow things down.

I built this Working Student Execution Framework to help myself stay reliable, organized, and transparent in day-to-day work. The goal was not productivity for its own sake, but clarity — for myself and for the people I work with.

The framework focuses on a few simple areas:

  • Tracking active tasks and priorities
  • Writing down open questions instead of keeping them in my head
  • Logging feedback to avoid repeating the same mistakes

Everything is kept lightweight so it can be used consistently without becoming another thing to maintain.

One part I found especially useful was separating questions from tasks. Instead of asking things randomly or forgetting them, I could collect questions, assign priority, and raise them at the right moment. This helped reduce confusion and made communication smoother.

Overall, this project reflects how I approach work as a student: take responsibility, communicate early, and improve step by step. It's designed for real team environments where reliability and learning matter more than complex systems.
